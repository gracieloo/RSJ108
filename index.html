1 Method to Creating a Good Caption in Photojournalism

Assembly time: 5-30 minutes
Awesome rating: 90% awesome

Crafting captivating photo captions is crucial in journalism. Readers often glance at photos and captions first, influencing their decision to delve into the full story. Follow these tips to create intriguing captions that entice readers to explore further.

  • Things You'll Need

  • The picture or pictures you are captioning
  • Some type of grammer checker
  • Writing device: pen, pencil, your keyboard
  • Paper, carboard, notebook, iPad, your mom's grocery receipt
  • A peer or friend to verify your facts and also check your grammer

  1. Verify your Information (facts)

    Accuracy is paramount in journalism; an incorrect detail undermines the credibility of your story or photo. Prior to publishing any photo captions, ensure that the information therein is accurate. If you face challenges in fact-checking, refrain from printing an inaccurate caption. It's preferable to omit information if its accuracy is uncertain, especially when pressed for time or lacking a reliable source.

  2. Don't state the Obvious

    Elevate your photo captions beyond the obvious. Merely describing the visuals is unproductive. For instance, captioning a sunset photo as "a sunset" adds little value. Instead, highlight less apparent details such as the location, time, or a specific event. Opt for descriptive captions like "Pacific coast sunset, March 2016, Long Beach, Vancouver Island." Additionally, steer clear of phrases like "is shown," "is pictured," "and looks on," or "above."

    Night falls over Notre-Dame de Paris

  3. Identify Important People

    Identify key individuals in the photo, providing their names if known (unless they've requested anonymity). If names are unknown, offer descriptive information like "protesters on the streets of Washington, DC." Ensure correct spelling and proper titles for all names used. If the photo features a group where not everyone is pertinent to the story, there's no obligation to name each individual in the caption

  4. Get Specific

    Prioritize specificity, closely tied to ensuring accuracy. Obtain details about the photo's location and individuals featured, ensuring meaningful context for the reader. Collaborate with fellow journalists if necessary. When identifying specific individuals, provide their location in the photo for clarity. You can structure your caption to begin broadly and become more specific, or start specific and transition to a broader context, ensuring both precision and readability.

  5. Give Historical Photos the Credit They Deserve

    Accurately categorize historical photos in your story, ensuring proper labeling with the date (at least the year) of capture. Provide due credit to the photographer and/or organization, depending on ownership (e.g., museum, archive, etc.).

  6. Stay in the Present

    Opt for the present tense in captions, especially for photos depicting current events. This choice conveys immediacy, enhancing the photo's impact on the reader. Reserve the past tense for appropriate use, such as with historical photos.

  7. Give Credit Where Its Due

    Ensure proper credits and citations for every photo, including the photographer's name or the owning organization. In photographic publications, consider including technical details like aperture, film speed, f-stop, and lens information. Maintain consistency in presenting credits, whether through italics, font size, or another format, eliminating the need for explicit terms like "credited to" or "photo by."
